**Year in review**

TechnologyGermany's AI surge: transforming industry and public sectors

Germany has long been a centre for technological innovation and AI research. Over the past 12 months, AI has gained traction in several of the country's key industrial sectors. Automotive giants such as Volkswagen, BMW and Mercedes-Benz employ AI for autonomous driving and predictive maintenance. In healthcare, AI has enhanced diagnostics, drug discovery and patient care. In manufacturing, companies are leveraging AI-enhanced robotics and other smart technologies in what has been termed the 'fourth industrial revolution' or 'Industry 4.0'. German banks and fintech companies utilise AI for fraud detection and algorithmic trading. The burgeoning and politically significant energy sector in Germany has seen AI-driven optimisations in renewable energy production and grid management. Finally, the German public sector is increasingly adopting AI for urban planning and traffic management.

Developments in policy and legislationGermany boosts AI research funding to €1 billion, aiming to catch global AI leaders

In August 2023, the German government announced that it plans to boost its AI research funding by nearly €1 billion over the next two years, aiming to catch up with AI leaders China and the United States. This initiative is part of Germany's broader economic rejuvenation efforts, particularly in areas such as the automotive and chemicals sectors. The plan includes establishing 150 new AI research labs at universities, investing in supercomputing infrastructure, providing support for women-led research groups and providing access to public datasets to stimulate AI development.<sup><a href="#footnotes_entry_016">3</a></sup>

Germany's funding commitment is now drawing level with the financial support pledged by the European Union. In this regard, the European Commission has dedicated €1 billion annually to AI research via the Horizon Europe programme. Furthermore, the Commission has outlined its intention to mobilise additional investments from both private sector sources and EU Member States, with the ultimate goal of achieving a yearly funding volume of €20 billion.<sup><a href="#footnotes_entry_015">4</a></sup>

While funding pledged by Germany's is still far behind the US$3.3 billion in public money that the United States reportedly threw at the field in 2022, German authorities emphasise the value inherent in their regulatory framework, which prioritises privacy and safety. This regulatory approach could attract AI stakeholders looking for a secure and compliant environment.

Federal Minister of Education and Research Bettina Stark-Watzinger presented the increased funding alongside Germany's AI Action Plan. The minister has argued that Germany should align its academic practices with its security interests, especially in light of tensions with countries that are considered systemic rivals, such as China.

CasesGerman court deems Palantir's police surveillance software unconstitutional

In February 2023, the German Federal Constitutional Court ruled against the use of Palantir's surveillance software by police in Hesse and Hamburg, deeming it unconstitutional. Palantir is a US data analytics firm that received early backing from intelligence agencies, including the Central Intelligence Agency, the Federal Bureau of Investigation and the National Security Agency, all in the United States. The case, initiated by the German Society for Civil Rights, argued that the software, dubbed 'Hessendata', facilitates predictive policing that could lead to mistakes and discrimination by law enforcement bodies. Although the software has been utilised in Hesse since 2017, it has not been deployed in Hamburg. The ruling came amid wider discussions in the German federal parliament regarding the use of AI tools by law enforcement, particularly the ongoing deliberations on the EU AI Act aimed at regulating AI use. The ruling requires Hesse to amend its legislation by 30 September, albeit with the software remaining in use subject to restrictions until then.<sup><a href="#footnotes_entry_014">5</a></sup>

German Federal Patent Court reverses stance: AI systems cannot be recognised as inventors, aligning with global patent office views

In June 2023 in the *Food container* case, the German Federal Patent Court held that, under German national case law, an AI system cannot be named as an inventor anywhere on a patent application.<sup><a href="#footnotes_entry_013">6</a></sup>

The underlying case concerned the neural network known as 'Device for the Autonomous Bootstrapping of Unified Sentience' (DABUS), an AI system created by famed American researcher and technologist Dr Stephen Thaler, which he claims is responsible for autonomous inventions. Over the past few years, various patent offices, including the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO) and the European Patent Office (EPO), had to take position on whether DABUS could be named as an inventor. The USPTO and the EPO both rejected the relevant patent applications, stating that they did not comply with the requirement that an inventor must be a natural person. Similarly, the patent offices of Australia, Israel, New Zealand and South Korea refused to accept Thaler's applications.<sup><a href="#footnotes_entry_012">7</a></sup>

The German decision is in line with the dominant trend in case law in these countries, yet, surprisingly, stands in contrast to the same court's previous stance in the same matter. In 2022, the court had allowed a designation of inventor where the applicant designated himself, with an addition that he had caused or prompted an AI system to generate the invention.<sup><a href="#footnotes_entry_011">8</a></sup> By doing so, the court allowed the applicant Stephen Thaler, who insisted on not having invented the food container, to be designated as the inventor, opening up an opportunity for him to become the patent owner. This decision was hailed by many patent practitioners in Germany, as it seemed to provide an answer to AI inventorship by enabling a human to be named as the deemed inventor while also recognising the creative contribution of the AI.
